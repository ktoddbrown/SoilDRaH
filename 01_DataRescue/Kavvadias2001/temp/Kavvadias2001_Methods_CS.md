##2. Materials and methods

##2.1 Study sites
The study was conducted on four undisturbed natural forest sites of maritime pine (*Pinus pinaster Aiton*), black pine (*Pinus nigra Arn.*), Bulgarian fir (*Abies borisii regis, Matf.*), and beech (*Fagus silvatica L.*).
The maritime pine site was derived from natural reforestation and the black pine site includes even aged stands derived from natural reforestation on abandoned eroded soils.
The fir and beech sites are comprised of uneven aged stands mature for harvesting.
The stand characteristics of the study sites are given in Table 1.
In each forest site, two square 0.5 ha plots were established on different site qualities, high (Hsq) and low site quality (Lsq).
Site qualities were determined according to @Apatsidis1990 and @Panagiotidis1981.
Ground vegetation in all sites was almost absent.

The maritime pine site was located in Kilkis (41°06′N, 22°53′E), approximately 70 km northwest of Thessaloniki with a semi-arid Mediterranean climate.
Average forest floor depth was 1–3 cm, and humus was of the xeromoder type (@Klinka1981).
The soil developed on gneiss and schists is shallow, well drained and acidic, with a sandy loam texture, and is classified as Eutric Cambisol (@FAO1988) (Table 2).
The black pine site (40°23′N, 22°32′E) was located on the northwest-facing slope of the Pieria mountains.
The climate is characterized as sub-Mediterranean to temperate.
The humus profile is of the mull-moder type at the Hsq and xeromoder at the Lsq (@Klinka1981).
Average forest floor depth ranged from 1.1 to 4.4 cm and overlayed an abandoned cultivated soil derived from the weathering of gneiss.
The soil was poor in organic matter, with a sandy loam texture.
The fir site was located in the University forest of Pertouli (39°30′N, 21°25′E) on Pindos mountain and the climate is characterized as sub-Mediterranean to temperate.
Humus at the two site qualities was of mull-moder type (@Klinka1981).
The mineral soil, formed on flysch parent material, was deep, acidic, and sandy loam in texture.
The beech site (39°50′N, 22°30′E) was located on the northeast-facing slope of mountain Ossa.
The climate is attenuated as sub-Mediterranean with rainy winters.
The forest floor was 1.5–5.4 cm thick and the humus was of the mull type at the Hsq and of the mull-moder type at the Lsq (@Klinka1981).
The soil derived from schists was, acidic, rich in organic matter, with a clay loam texture.
Soils under the black pine, fir and beech sites are classified as Dystric Cambisols (@FAO1988).

##3. Sample collection

##3.1 Litter fall

Litter fall was collected from the beginning of January 1994 and extended to December 1995 at monthly intervals.
Twelve litter traps, each of 0.5 m$^2$, were randomly erected in each plot.
Each trap consisted of 2 mm mesh nylon netting (on a wooden frame) suspended from a wire hoop and held 40 cm above the ground by three metal poles.
At the four stands, the above ground litter input was composed of 95–97% leaves and 3–5% reproductive structures (seeds, cones, flower parts) and branch bark.

##3.2 Forest Floor

Forest floor sampling took place in December 1994 and 1995.
Accumulation of litter on the ground was determined on 35 randomly distributed positions within each plot.
Samples of forest floor were obtained by pressing a 625 cm$^2$ steel sheet sampling frame (10 cm deep) into the forest floor and collecting all organic material above the mineral soil.
In each of these samples the horizons L and F, H were separated and carefully placed in plastic bags.
The L horizon is composed of fresh or slightly discoloured, with no or weak breaking up, material.
The F horizon is composed of medium to strongly fragmented material with many mycelia and thin roots and the H horizon is a humified amorphous material (@Papamichos1990).
Mineral soil was removed after successive sieving.
All litter fall and forest floor samples were oven-dried to constant weight at 70°C and weighed.
Litter subsamples were then ground in a Willey mill to pass a 40 mesh stainless steel sieve before being analyzed.

##3.3 Mineral soil

On each stand seven randomly selected samples were collected from the different horizons and physical and chemical analyses were performed on the composite samples.

##3.4 Chemical analyses

All soil samples were air dried and sieved through 2 mm mesh screens. 
Particle size distribution of mineral soil was determined according to @Bouyoucos1962 and the pH of the mineral soil was determined on a soil: water suspension (1:1, by weight) using a glass electrode.
Soil organic matter was determined by means of wet oxidation (@Walkley1934, @Walkley1947).
Total N was determined by the Kjeldahl method (@Bremmer1965).
Available P was extracted with 0.5 N NaHCO$_3$ at pH 8.5 and was measured spectrophotometrically by a modified phospomolybdenum blue method (@Alifragis1995).
The exchangeable cations Ca, Mg, K and Na were extracted from soil samples using 1 N ammonium acetate (pH 7).
The extracted cations were then measured by atomic absorption spectrophotometry.
Litter subsamples were combusted in a muffle furnace at 520°C for 4 h and diluted with HCl in a 1:1 ratio v/v (@Allen1974).
The solutions were analyzed for total P colorimetrically using the phospomolybdenum blue method, and for total Ca, Mg, K and Na by atomic absorption spectroscopy.
Total N was determined by Kjeldahl analysis.
The organic matter was determined by loss on ignition at 540°C for 5 h.

##3.5 Residence time computatuons and statistical analyses

The number of years for which dry matter, organic matter and nutrient elements reside in the forest floor was estimated using @Olson1963 formula (*k*=Lss/Fss) where Lss is the mass of the annual litter input and Fss the mass of the forest floor.
The reciprocal indicates the turnover time in years for the forest floor.
The calculations are based on the assumption that steady-state conditions for a given litter type with respect to input and losses exist.
Because site quality was not replicated within a particular forest type, the experimental design for the statistical analyses of forest floor data was a randomized split plot design with four replicates (Table 3, Table 4).
Forest types were the actual replicates, with the two site qualities embedded within each block as main plots.
Within each site quality and forest type were two soil horizons as split plots.
In addition, within each forest type differences in litter fall and its chemical composition between the two site qualities were tested using unpaired t-test.
All statistical analyses were performed using SPSS software package.
