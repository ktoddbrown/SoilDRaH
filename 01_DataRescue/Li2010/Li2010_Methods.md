# Methods

## Study sites

The study was conducted along the Hamakua coast (19°30′N, 155°15′W) of the Big Island of Hawaii.
The study area is characterized by a wet tropical climate.
Mean monthly precipitation and temperature during the study period were 320 mm and 73.9°F, respectively (Fig. 1).
The sites are relatively flat with a slope of <15% degrees and an elevation of about 300 m above sea level.
The silty clay loam soils are classified as hydrous, ferrihydritic, Acrudoxic Hydrudands of the Akaka and Kaiwiki series.
These soils that developed from volcanic ash and cinders are dominated by amorphous minerals such as ferrihydrite and poorly crystalline Al oxide, hydroxide, and hydrous oxide phases (including hydroxy-Al polymers and nearly non-crystalline gibbsite), but are non-allophanic (<13% allophone + imogolite) [@Parfitt1988; @PatrickPersComm].

The secondary forests and pastures were established on sugarcane farmlands that were abandoned in 1985 when the sugarcane production became unprofitable.
These sugarcane lands had been fertilized with N (85 kg ha$^{−1}$ year$^{−1}$), P (75 kg ha$^{−1}$ year$^{−1}$), and K (110 kg ha$^{−1}$ year$^{−1}$) since 1920 [@Bashkin1998].
All sites were within 5 km of each other, with similar land use history and characteristics.

The secondary forests are dominated by dense stands of *Terminalia myriocarpa*, *Psidium Cattleianum*, and *Spathodea campanulata*.
The average tree height was about 6 to 8 m and the average diameter at breast height (DBH; 1.3 m) was 10 to 15 cm.
The vegetation in the pastures was dominated by *Brachiaria mutica*, *Cyperus gracilis*, and *Panicum maximum*.
The pastures voluntarily established via natural regeneration without seeding or other facilitation.
The pastures were grazed by cattle (*Bos* spp.) but not mechanically harvested.

## Field measurement and laboratory

During August 2007 three soil samples were collected from the 0–10 and 10–25 cm soil depths at each of the forest and pasture sites for analysis of chemical and physical properties.
Each of the three samples consisted of a well-mixed composite of three soil cores (18.9 mm in diameter).
Approximately 5 gram subsamples for C isotope analysis were taken from each soil sample and placed in separate plastic bags.
Soil pH, exchangeable Ca, Mg, and K, extractable P, and total C and N analyses were conducted at the Agricultural Diagnostic Service Center–University of Hawaii at Manoa.
The soil isotope, exchangeable Na and Al, and amorphous and poorly crystalline Fe and Al analyses were performed in the Marine Sciences Analytical Laboratory of the University of Hawaii at Hilo.

Total C and N were determined by dry combustion in a LECO CHN2000 elemental analyzer.
The concentration of extractable P was obtained using the Modified-Truog (0.02 N H$_2$SO$_4$ + 0.04 N [NH$_4$]$_2$SO$_4$) extraction procedure followed by colorimetric analysis using an autoanalyzer [@Hue2000].
Concentrations of exchangeable K, Ca, and Mg were determined using a Thermal Jarell Ash Inductively Coupled Plasma Analyzer (ICP) after samples were extracted with neutral 1 N NH$_4$OAc [@Hue2000].
Exchangeable Al and Na were extracted with NH$_4$Cl followed by analysis by ICP [@Shuman1990].
Effective cation exchange capacity (ECEC) was calculated as the sum of exchangeable cations (K + Ca + Mg + Na + Al) expressed on a cmol$_c$ kg$^{−1}$ basis while Al saturation percentage was calculated as cmol$_c$ kg$^{−1}$ exchangeable Al divided by ECEC and multiplied by 100.
Amorphous and poorly crystalline Fe and Al were extracted with acid (pH 3.0) ammonium oxalate followed by elemental analysis by ICP [@Parfitt1989].
Soil pH was measured in a 1:1 ratio of water to soil fresh weight with a combination electrode.
Soil moisture contents were determined by oven-drying 10 g of fresh soil at 105°C for 48 h.
Bulk density of soil was determined on core samples which are taken by driving a corer into the soil at the desired depth.
The samples are then oven dried and weighed.
Bulk density = mass of oven dry soil/core volume.
Plant samples for isotopic analysis were collected from fresh leaves and oven dried for 96 h at 60°C.

δ $^{13}$C in plant samples and soils were analyzed using high temperature combustion in an Elemental Analyzer (Costech ECS 4010) which separates CO$_2$ and N$_2$ on a GC column.
The effluent from the GC column in a Helium carrier gas is introduced into a Continuous Flow Interface to an Isotope Ratio Mass Spectrometer (CF-IRMS).
A Thermo-Finnegan Delta V Advantage Mass Spectrometer with a ConfloIII interface was used.
Quantification of weight % C and N is accomplished using a standard curve of Acetanilide (Acros 15081-5000, CAS#103-84-4) or Glycine (Alfa Aesar 36435, CAS# 56-40-6).
Peach Leaves (NIST-1547) were also used as a lab standard for plant sample analysis.
Certified standards used in the stable isotopic analysis of C are NBS-22 (oil) and NBS-19 (CaCO$_3$).
δ $^{13}$C values are referenced against the VPDB (Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite carbonate, δ $^{13}$C=0) international standard.
Data are expressed as δ $^{13}$C in units of parts per thousand (‰) = $$ [(R_{sample} - R_{std})/R_{std}] \times 1000 $$; where ratio *R*=$^{13}$C/$^{12}$C.

## Calculation and data analysis

The percentage of total soil organic C coming from sugarcane and grasses (C$_4$) was calculated as [@Resh2002].:

$$ \%SOC_4 = [(δ^{13} C_{sample} - δ^{13}C_3) / (δ^{13} C_4 - δ^{13}C_3)] \space (1) $$

$$ \%SOC_3 = 100 - \%SOC_4 \space (2) $$

where δ $^{13}$C$_{sample}$ is the δ $^{13}$C value of soil sample, δ $^{13}$C$_3$ is the δ $^{13}$C value of pure forest litter, and δ $^{13}$C$_4$ is the δ $^{13}$C value of a composite sample of cane litter and roots and grass litter.

The percentages of %SOC$_4$ and %SOC$_3$ were multiplied by TC (total C) to obtain C (Mg ha$^{−1}$) derived from the secondary forest (SOC$_3$) and cane/ grass vegetation (SOC$_4$).
The net SOC$_3$ and SOC$_4$ changes in the soils at 0–10 cm and 10–25 cm were calculated as the difference of soil C pool between forest and pasture.
These calculations were based on the following assumptions: A) Most grasses in the pasture were C$_4$ species and therefore the SOC$_4$ increment can be assumed from pasture, and B) Most of forest plants were C$_3$ although there was some C$_4$ understory vegetation.
Significant differences among means of treatments were determined using two-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s test at α=0.05.
